Copper (Cu)

The Essential Metal for Our Modern World

by Aprameyan Anantha and Jaivik Bhogal

Physical & Chemical Properties

Copper is a reddish-brown metal with excellent electrical and thermal conductivity. It's malleable and ductile, perfect for wiring and shaping.

Group & Atomic Structure

Copper belongs to Group 11 (Transition Metals).

Atomic Structure:

  • Protons (#p+): 29
  • Electrons (#e-): 29
  • Neutrons (#no): 34

Mining & Processing

Copper is mined in Chile, the U.S. (Utah, Arizona), Peru, and Canada using open-pit or underground mining. It’s processed through smelting to obtain pure copper.

Role in Cell Phones

Copper’s excellent conductivity makes it critical for wiring, connections, and circuitry in cell phones, enabling fast transmission of data and power.

Social Impact of Cell Phones

Pros

  • Instant connectivity and communication
  • Access to information and education
  • Entertainment and mental stimulation
  • Health and safety apps (GPS, emergency calls)

Cons

  • Increased anxiety and depression
  • Addiction and distraction
  • Sleep disruption from blue light
  • Privacy concerns and data risks

Health Hazards in Mining

Mining releases toxic dust and gases causing respiratory diseases. Workers face risky conditions. Mining in India and Nigeria has caused conflicts and corruption. Groups like the Bench Marks Foundation advocate for miners’ rights.

Environmental Impacts

  • Mining destroys ecosystems and causes soil erosion.
  • Charging phones produces carbon emissions.
  • Improper disposal causes toxic metal leakage into soil and water.

Economic Impacts of Cell Phones

Manufacturing cell phones is expensive due to costly raw materials. Although profitable, the industry also causes hidden environmental and human costs.

Importance of Recycling

Recycling phones conserves copper and other resources, reduces mining, and helps protect the planet while saving energy and lowering pollution.
